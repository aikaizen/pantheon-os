# Executive Summary — Hermes
### Enterprise Agent Operating System

**Date**: March 2026
**Status**: Internal — Strategic Direction
**Stage**: Transition from consultancy to enterprise software company

---

## What We Are

We are an enterprise software company that deploys an **Agent Operating System (AgentOS)** — the configuration and infrastructure layer that makes human + agent collaboration reliable, governed, and cumulative inside large organizations.

We are not a consultancy. We are not an AI vendor selling capability.

We sell **operational outcomes**: workflows that don't drift, institutional knowledge that doesn't disappear, agent operations that senior buyers can trust and audit.

Our go-to-market uses **forward deployment teams** — embedded operators who configure the system inside the client's environment, map their workflows, and make adoption real. This is the Palantir model applied to agent orchestration. The team is the product until the product is self-sufficient. That is a feature, not a liability.

---

## The Core Problem We Solve

Enterprise teams that try to adopt AI fail in a predictable pattern:

1. They buy capability (a model, a chatbot, an automation tool)
2. The capability has no memory of how the organization works
3. The agents operate without governance or visibility
4. Work runs for a few weeks, then drifts or breaks silently
5. No one knows what the agent touched, what it decided, or why

The failure is not the AI. The failure is the absence of an operating system underneath it.

Every organization that wants agents to work reliably needs three things:

| Layer | Question it answers | Our product |
|-------|-------------------|-------------|
| Memory | What does the organization know? | Institutional Memory Engine |
| Workflow | How does the organization work? | Long-Horizon Workflow Agent |
| Control | Who decides what, and with what authority? | Agent Runtime Control Plane |

Together these form the **Company OS** — the substrate that makes human + agent collaboration trustworthy, auditable, and cumulative. This is the repeatable core we productize and deploy.

## The Product Stack

### Layer 1 — Institutional Memory Engine
Structured memory system that captures, governs, and retrieves operational knowledge for both humans and agents.

**What it replaces**: knowledge trapped in chat threads, consultant heads, docs no one can find, and meeting notes that never become reusable systems.

**Why it matters first**: Memory makes every subsequent product and every subsequent engagement cumulative. Without it, every deployment starts from zero.

**Enterprise value**: onboarding new staff faster, preserving knowledge through transitions, giving agents reliable and permission-safe context instead of brittle prompts.

### Layer 2 — Long-Horizon Workflow Agent
An agent designed to keep multi-week, multi-stakeholder workflows moving without drift, missed follow-up, or invisible blockers.

**What it replaces**: status spread across tools, no clear next action, deadlines that slip silently, ownership changes that are never reflected in the system.

**Enterprise value**: onboarding programs, delivery coordination, compliance work, recruiting pipelines, strategic projects with multiple milestones and owners — any process where the cost of failure accumulates over weeks.

### Layer 3 — Agent Runtime Control Plane
A control plane for operating agents and automations safely across multiple workflows, teams, and environments.

**What it replaces**: no visibility into what agents are running, no approval paths for risky actions, no cost visibility by client or workflow, no audit trail.

**Enterprise value**: governance, compliance, trust. The layer that makes a CFO or CTO comfortable signing an enterprise contract. Becomes essential once more than one agent is active.

### The Stack Is the Moat

Each layer is useful alone. Together they are significantly harder to displace:
- Memory gives the platform knowledge of the client's organization
- Workflow gives the platform behavioral knowledge of how the client operates
- Control makes the platform the system of record for agent governance

The longer a client uses the stack, the more expensive it is to leave. This is switching-cost defensibility built from genuine operational integration, not contractual lock-in.

## Strategic Trade-offs

**We choose enterprise depth over SMB breadth.** Large organizations with operational complexity get the full value of the stack. SMB deals are not worth the forward deployment cost.

**We choose repeatability over novelty.** Every custom engagement is evaluated for what can be standardized. We reject work that cannot feed the product roadmap.

**We choose outcomes over capability.** We do not sell "AI." We sell: missed follow-up reduced, reporting time reduced, project drift reduced, knowledge reuse increased.

**We choose forward deployment over no-touch.** Especially in the Middle East and for first-time enterprise deployments. We earn trust through operational proximity, then reduce it as the product becomes self-sufficient.

**We choose a proven stack over a platform bet.** We build Memory → Workflow → Control Plane in sequence, not all at once. Each must be live and earning before we build the next.

## Go-to-Market Model

### The Delivery Motion

```
Diagnostic Sprint  →  Fixed-Scope Pilot  →  Forward-Deployed Implementation  →  Subscription + Managed Service
```

1. **Diagnostic Sprint** — paid, fixed scope. Maps workflows, identifies bottlenecks, produces ROI model and 90-day recommendation. Generates trust and pipeline.

2. **Pilot** — fixed fee, scoped success criteria. One workflow improved end-to-end. Measurable outcome. Proves the model.

3. **Forward-Deployed Implementation** — embedded team configures memory, workflow, and governance inside the client's environment. This is where the product becomes real for that account.

4. **Subscription + Managed Service** — ongoing software subscription (per-workflow, per-operator, or per-module) plus optional retained managed service for high-value accounts.

### Who We Sell To

**Best-fit buyers**:
- Enterprise and large mid-market organizations ($50M+ revenue)
- Operations, delivery, and transformation leaders with measurable workflow pain
- Organizations running multi-team, multi-week, multi-system work
- Buyers who already understand the cost of coordination failure

**Second-tier buyers (worth pursuing in later stages)**:
- Large family businesses with operational complexity (especially Gulf region)
- Professional services firms with delivery overhead problems
- Logistics, construction, healthcare, and shared services operations

**Explicitly not pursuing**:
- Startups and SMBs (insufficient complexity for the stack)
- Buyers seeking AI novelty with no operational problem
- Teams without process ownership or mandate to change

## Pricing Direction

### Principles
- Price on value (outcomes), not on effort (hours)
- Each tier should produce enough revenue to justify forward deployment cost
- Subscription pricing should reflect operational depth, not seat count alone

### Indicative Pricing Structure

| Offer | Model | Range |
|-------|-------|-------|
| Diagnostic Sprint | Fixed fee | $15,000 – $30,000 |
| Pilot Implementation | Fixed fee, success-scoped | $40,000 – $100,000 |
| Forward-Deployed Implementation | Time-and-scope, per engagement | $75,000 – $250,000 |
| Annual Software Subscription | Per workflow + per operator tier | $120,000 – $600,000+ |
| Managed Service Retainer | Monthly recurring | $20,000 – $60,000/month |

### Subscription Metric Options
Tie annual subscription pricing to one or more of:
- number of active workflows under management
- number of operator seats using the platform
- number of active agent configurations
- memory workspace count (for Memory Engine module)
- number of client workspaces (for agencies/operators running the stack on behalf of their clients)

### Revenue Mix Target
The goal is progressive migration away from pure services:

| Phase | Services | Recurring Software/Retainer |
|-------|----------|---------------------------|
| Now (Phase 1) | 80% | 20% |
| 12–18 months (Phase 2) | 55% | 45% |
| 24–36 months (Phase 3) | 30% | 70% |

This is not a race to eliminate services. Services feed the roadmap and make enterprise adoption work. The goal is to make services higher-margin advisory work over time, not to eliminate them.

## Middle East Strategy

The Middle East — specifically UAE and Saudi Arabia — represents the clearest near-term regional opportunity given the combination of transformation budgets, operational complexity at enterprise scale, and forward deployment as the natural sales motion.

### Why this model fits the region
- Large-scale transformation programs with operational drag: high
- Tolerance for embedded, relationship-led delivery: high
- Demand for governance and auditability (especially in regulated sectors): high
- Preference for face-to-face credibility over cold SaaS demos: strongly true

### Recommended Sequence

**Step 1 — UAE as commercial base**
- Fastest market for pilots, references, and partner relationships
- English-led sales motion works
- Internationally oriented enterprise buyers are accessible
- Strong event and partnership density

**Step 2 — Saudi as strategic expansion**
- Highest upside of any Gulf market
- More relationship-intensive and procurement-heavy
- Requires local presence or serious local partner
- Best for large workflow transformation programs, government-adjacent work, and large family group enterprises
- Enter Saudi with UAE references, not ambition alone

### Regional Sectors (Priority Order)
1. Logistics and supply chain operations
2. Real estate and construction delivery management
3. Healthcare operations (non-clinical workflow)
4. Government-adjacent transformation programs
5. Large enterprise shared services and support operations
6. Complex family business group operations

### Operational Requirements for the Region
- Arabic executive materials (one-pager, proposal template, executive summary)
- English primary sales deck
- Clear data residency and hosting options (UAE or Saudi-resident if required)
- Local legal entity or in-country partner before large public sector or regulated-sector work
- After-sales responsiveness model that matches regional expectations
